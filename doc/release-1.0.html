Release Notes for Mylar 1.0

Mylar’s task-focused user interface is a new technology that represents a fundamental shift in the way IDEs work. As the complexity of applications has grown, IDEs have made it easier to browse and edit the structure of these systems. Content assist provided instant access to that modularity in the editor, while refactoring has facilitated making structural changes.  As the size of systems has continued to grow IDEs' structure views have become overloaded with thousands of elements. Searches often return hundreds of hits.  The resulting information overload has been causing us to waste a tremendous amount of time browsing, scrolling, and navigating to identify the information we need. What’s worse is that we’re constantly multi-tasking, and whenever we switch tasks we waste yet more time re-creating this context.

The key intuition behind Mylar is that for any task that we do, any bug that we need to fix or feature we add, we only care about a subset of the system. Mylar makes this subset explicit by automatically managing task context on our behalf.  The result is that structure views show only the relevant information, changes are grouped by task, multi-tasking only requires a single click, and expertise becomes easy to share. 

History

In 2003, I was working on integrating new modularity technologies with IDEs. Aspect-oriented programming tools like AspectJ, as well as model-driven development tools, can remove significant amounts of the accidental complexity that creeps into our systems. But even with improved modularity, it became clear to me that the essential complexity of systems would continue growing, while our ability to deal with that complexity would not. I knew that Gail Murphy, a professor at the University of British Columbia, had a deep appreciation for this problem and would have a uniquely practical and empirical approach to finding a solution as evidenced by the FEAT and Hipikat research projects. I had a feeling that this problem could be solved with her guidance, and she convinced me that the best way to find a solution was by doing a PhD.

I have loved programming since I was ten years old, when I learned English from a Sinclair ZX1000 manual. At Xerox PARC got infected with a passion for building tools that make people more productive. What would sometimes slow me down is RSI that resulted from very long workdays. The start of my thesis gave me a chance to reflect on what the underlying problem was: I was constantly clicking the mouse and keyboard to find and re-find the information I needed to get work done. What I hadn’t noticed before is that I was always browsing for the same elements, and that the IDE was showing them within a deep hierarchy instead of grouping them in a more reasonable way. Gail and I brainstormed weekly on how the structure relevant to developers’ tasks could be identified and presented. She instilled in me the need to validate any whacky ideas that we came up with via user studies, and in August of 2004 we installed Mylar 0.1 on six IBM developers’ desktops. We measured their usage patterns before and after they had Mylar, and found that when the IDE views were focused on task, they would spend more time programming and less time clicking around looking for the information they needed to program.

We presented Mylar 0.2 at the EclipseCon 2005 conference in order to find more study subjects. This caught the eye of Bjorn-Freeman Benson, who got the idea instantly, and suggested that we propose Mylar as an eclipse.org project. On July 4th, 2005 we released Mylar 0.3 to the first hundred developers who were willing to participate in our user study. Four months later we had collected enough data to validate with statistical significance that Mylar makes programmers more productive. Thanks to the constant releases and the fact that all work done on Mylar was done with Mylar, we had produced a reasonably well-integrated tool that we were happy to share with others. Mylar 0.4 was our first public release on eclipse.org, and happened on November 4th, 2005.

At that point, an amazing thing happened, and has continued happening since. Developers kept getting addicted to Mylar. Like us, they couldn’t imagine working without it, and became as passionate about having it support their workday as we were. A steady stream of bug reports started rolling in, and defined how programmers needed Mylar to work in order to support their development process. Many bug reports were accompanied by patches that helped Mylar evolve to meet these ever increasing needs. Since then we have resolved 1826 bug reports, over 200 of which were resolved by community contributions.

The result is Mylar 1.0, which integrates task context seamlessly across the Eclipse IDE, and brings developers’ tasks into Eclipse via connectors to the Bugzilla, JIRA, Trac, as well as other web-based repositories. More importantly, Mylar 1.0 provides frameworks to integrate task context with other kinds of artifacts and task repositories, enabling future versions of Mylar to bring the benefits of task context to a rapidly growing number of developers.

The IBM Centre for Advanced Studies and NSERC funding of my PhD made it possible for me to focus all of my energy on the implementation and validation of Mylar.
